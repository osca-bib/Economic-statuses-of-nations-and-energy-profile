    Project Description

    This project analyzes the relationship between global economic output, energy consumption, and renewable energy adoption using two CSV data sources on global energy and GDP.

    The visualizations are grouped into three sections: economic Scale and energy, efficiency and regional Trends, and Energy Change over Time.

    Some key insights reveal a strong link between GDP and total energy consumption, but demonstrate a weak correlation between wealth and energy efficiency or renewable energy adoption.

    Furthermore, although renewable energy has grown rapidly over the past two decades, non-renewable sources continue to dominate the primary energy supply, showing that economic growth alone does not drive the transition to clean energy.

    Main Findings and Takeaways

    Through this project, we had four key insights based on our visualizations.

    Firstly, a country's total energy consumption has a strong power relation to economic output, and a weak relation to energy efficiency and renewable adoption. Meaning that economic growth results in heightened demand for energy, but it does not lead countries to pursue cleaner and more efficient forms of energy, implying that other factors, such as a nation's politics, could have an impact.

    Secondly, countries energy efficiency could be influenced by its neighbors in their region. This can be seen in energy-efficient regions such as Western Europe, juxtaposing energy-inefficient nations in the former Soviet Union.

    Thirdly, clean energy is rapidly expanding through newer energy sources in solar and wind, in contrast to hydro, meaning that progress towards renewable energy is being led by newer technological growth instead of building upon older systems.

    Fourthly, despite renewable energy's growth, demand for non-renewable energy has exceeded renewable energy. This indicates that the world is currently using renewable energy to supplement, rather than replace, fossil fuels to meet rising global energy demands.

    Overall, we have found that for the world to move away from nonrenewable energy, nations must not be passive in their battle for clean energy, but instead be aggressive, pushing for improved policy towards renewable energy and lowered total energy demands.
