    Economic Scale and Energy

    Exploring the correlation between a nation's wealth, energy consumption, and renewable energy adoption.

    GDP vs. Total Energy Consumption

    This graph is a scatter plot on a logarithmic scale, plotting the GDP of countries against their total energy consumption. The visualization reveals a strong positive logarithmic relationship between a country's total GDP and its energy consumption. This suggests a power relationship between GDP and energy usage: as GDP grows, energy usage tends to increase exponentially. Furthermore, the plot shows that countries with similar GDPs can have significantly different total energy usage, suggesting that wealth is a key but not the sole in factor determining a nation's energy use.

    Correlation Matrix (GDP, Energy, Renewable Share)

    This is a correlation matrix, analyzing the relationship between GDP, total energy consumption, renewable energy percentage, and energy per capita. It numerically supports the visual trend seen in "GDP vs. Total Energy Consumption" above, with a high correlation of 89% between GDP and total energy consumption. Crucially, the matrix shows a -7% correlation between total energy consumption and renewable energy share, indicating that countries consuming more energy do not tend to have a higher proportion of renewable-based energy. Additionally, the -5% correlation between GDP and the percentage of renewable energy demonstrates that nations with more advanced economies have not yet fully committed to shifting towards green energy.

    Energy Demanded per Capita: Above vs. Below Average GDP

    This figure uses a violin visualization to compare the per capita energy usage of nations with above-average and below-average GDPs. The graph reveals that, on average, nations with above-average GDP utilize significantly more energy per capita. It also demonstrates that nations with below-average GDP have a very diverse spread of energy per capita usage (a wider violin shape), suggesting a lack of strong correlation between per capita energy use and GDP at the lower economic scale. However, many higher-GDP nations show per capita energy usage equivalent to lower-GDP nations, further complicating the overall correlation between these two variables.
